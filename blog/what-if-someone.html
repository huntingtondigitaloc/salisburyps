COVID-19 Update

What If Someone In Your Family Tests Positive?

Total COVID cases, hospitalizations and deaths (from NY Times).

There are early signs that Omicron has begun to peak. A huge surge in cases that lasts for about one month, followed by a rapid decline, would be consistent with the experience in South Africa and the UK where Omicron arrived earlier than in the U.S.

By now, we all know people who have tested positive for COVID. The massive number of people infected with Omicron has pushed daily caseloads to record levels and overwhelmed emergency rooms and hospitals, even though all evidence shows that while being more contagious, Omicron is less likely than Delta to produce as severe an illness. Massachusetts and California are reporting record numbers of COVID-19 infections for multiple days in a row. To help comprehend the scope of this pandemic, let us take a closer look at the numbers in California. California has close to 40,000,000 people, and by now, more than 6 million have tested positive for COVID, which represents 15% of the total population. We both know vaccine doubters who have tested positive in recent weeks, have gotten seriously ill, and are now willing to accept their vaccines. We have all heard of fully vaccinated and boosted people getting infected with COVID but what do the numbers actually tell us? As you can see above, the number of COVID cases amongst the vaccinated and boosted are rising but two facts remain clear – the chance of contracting a COVID infection is much higher in the unvaccinated, and the scary part is that the unvaccinated are much more likely to get severely ill or die from an infection. These numbers give us a compelling reason to get vaccinated and to stay boosted. In the future, the virus will continue to mutate and vaccines will have to be reformulated for the new variants – Pfizer announced that it will be manufacturing 100 million doses of an Omicron-specific COVID-19 vaccine.

We have seen instances where one family member or coworker gets ill with COVID but does not pass the disease onto others with whom they have had close contact. For a disease as apparently contagious as COVID-19, particularly the Omicron variant, it is difficult to comprehend how only one of two coworkers or family members, spending time together unmasked, while one was beginning to show symptoms but had not tested positive yet, gets ill while the other does not. The question arises as to what we should do if a coworker or family member tests positive for COVID. The CDC recommends that fully vaccinated and boosted persons who are exposed to another person with COVID do not have to quarantine but should wear a mask for ten days, get tested on the fifth day and only stay home if they do test positive. Those unvaccinated or not fully boosted should quarantine immediately by staying home and getting tested on the 5th day, and continue wearing a mask around others for another five days. Any household member who has tested positive should quarantine in a specific “sick room” – the CDC has just shortened the recommended isolation time from ten days to five but the infected person should wear a mask for a full ten days when around anyone else. If possible, the infected person should use a separate bathroom, wash hands frequently, clean and disinfect surfaces frequently, and avoid sharing household items with the unvaccinated. The CDC is also asking everyone to wear surgical or N95 masks instead of those made of cloth – yes, they cost more but are 75 times more effective at preventing infection with COVID-19 than surgical masks. But be careful when ordering on the internet - 60% of N95 masks are counterfeit!

Two frightening situations we encountered this past week further highlight the dangers of the COVID era. First of all, we encountered a patient who related that a few weeks earlier, they had flown to Miami to board a cruise but were denied entry because one of the partners tested positive for COVID. The couple quarantined in a hotel for ten days as recommended, after which the infected person tested negative for COVID, but now the other partner tested positive – they were tired of quarantining and did the unthinkable. They denied COVID contact or symptoms when checking in with the airlins - yes, they did wear masks, but they boarded a plane and flew home to complete their quarantine – thus risking the lives of all of the people they encountered on their way to and from the airport, at the airport and on the airplane, for their convenience! The other case involved a totally asymptomatic patient who tested positive on a presurgical screening the day before a scheduled procedure – we test all surgical patients just for this reason, to prevent spreading the virus to surgery center personnel and other patients. Be vigilant, stay safe out there!
